# AGENTS.md

## Product Overview

This project is a secure, intelligent task management system for teams that need a reliable place to organize work, understand priorities, and get helpful answers about their tasks.

The product should feel like a professional workspace where people can confidently create, assign, track, discuss, search, and understand work without feeling overwhelmed. The final experience should be simple enough for a new user to understand quickly, but polished enough to show that privacy, security, usefulness, and accessibility were considered from the beginning.

## Why This Project Exists

Teams often lose time because task information is scattered, unclear, duplicated, or hard to find. A user may know that something was discussed or assigned, but not remember where it is, who owns it, what changed, or what should happen next.

This product exists to reduce that friction. It should help users quickly answer questions like:

- What work needs my attention?
- What is the status of a task?
- Who owns this item?
- Are there similar or duplicate tasks already?
- What changed recently?
- What should I focus on next?
- Can I trust that I am only seeing information I am allowed to see?

The goal is not only to store tasks. The goal is to make work easier to understand and safer to manage.

## Client Perspective

From the client perspective, this project should demonstrate a complete, thoughtful product that could realistically become a production application.

The client wants to see that the product is:

- Useful for real teams, not just a technical demo.
- Easy to understand during a short review.
- Secure by default, especially when handling private team data.
- Polished enough to show care in user experience.
- Reliable enough that basic flows do not break during evaluation.
- Clear about what users can do and what they cannot do.
- Built with enough structure that it could grow after the assessment.

The final product should make the reviewer feel that the system was designed intentionally, not assembled randomly.

## Customer Perspective

From the customer perspective, the product should feel calm, clear, and trustworthy.

A customer should be able to sign in, view their workspace, manage tasks, and get helpful assistance without needing to understand how the system works behind the scenes.

Customers should feel that:

- Their information is private.
- They only see work they are allowed to see.
- The system helps them move faster.
- The interface is easy to navigate.
- Important actions are clear before they happen.
- Errors are understandable and recoverable.
- The assistant gives helpful answers without exposing sensitive information.
- Accessibility was considered so the product works for different users and situations.

The product should never feel confusing, risky, or careless with user data.

## What The End Product Should Look Like

At the end, the product should look like a clean team task workspace with intelligent assistance built in.

A user should be able to:

- Sign in securely.
- See a dashboard of relevant work.
- Create and manage tasks.
- Assign ownership and track progress.
- View task details clearly.
- Understand recent activity.
- Ask questions about their tasks and receive grounded, useful answers.
- Get help identifying possible duplicate tasks.
- Trust that private or restricted information is protected.

The interface should feel modern, simple, and professional. It should avoid clutter and guide users toward the next useful action.

The most important product impression should be:

> This is a secure task management workspace that helps teams understand and manage their work faster.

## Product Principles

### 1. Trust Comes First

Users must be able to trust the product with private task information. Any feature that risks exposing the wrong information should be treated as incomplete until it is safe.

### 2. Clarity Over Complexity

The product should prefer clear, simple flows over impressive but confusing behavior. Users should always understand what they are looking at and what action they can take next.

### 3. Helpfulness Without Guessing Too Much

Intelligent features should assist users, not pretend to know more than they do. When the system is unsure, it should be honest and cautious.

### 4. Accessibility Is Part Of Quality

The product should be usable by people with different needs, devices, and interaction styles. Keyboard navigation, readable content, clear focus, meaningful labels, and understandable errors are part of the product experience.

### 5. Security Should Be Invisible But Present

The user should not have to think about security constantly, but the product should consistently protect their information in the background.

### 6. Every Screen Should Have A Purpose

Each page, message, form, and action should help the user complete real work. Avoid adding features or content that do not improve the customer experience.

### 7. The Demo Should Tell A Story

The final product should be easy to demonstrate as a complete flow:

1. A user signs in.
2. They view their work.
3. They create or inspect tasks.
4. They ask for help understanding their tasks.
5. The product gives useful, safe, relevant assistance.
6. The user leaves with more clarity than they had before.

## Desired User Experience

The product should feel:

- Calm
- Secure
- Organized
- Helpful
- Fast enough for normal use
- Easy to navigate
- Respectful of user privacy
- Professional and assessment-ready

Avoid experiences that feel:

- Overcomplicated
- Noisy
- Fragile
- Inconsistent
- Visually unfinished
- Unclear about permissions
- Unsafe with private data

## Important User Expectations

Users expect that task management basics work reliably:

- They can find their tasks.
- They can understand task status.
- They can see who is responsible.
- They can recover from mistakes.
- They receive clear feedback after actions.
- They are not shown information that does not belong to them.

Users also expect the intelligent assistant to be useful but controlled:

- It should answer based on available task information.
- It should not invent private details.
- It should not reveal restricted information.
- It should explain when it cannot answer.
- It should guide users back to the product when needed.

## Definition Of A Successful Final Product

The project is successful when a reviewer can use it and understand its value within a few minutes.

A successful final product should show:

- A complete task management experience.
- Clear user flows.
- Responsible handling of private information.
- Intelligent assistance that feels practical.
- Strong attention to security and accessibility.
- A polished enough interface to feel intentional.
- A smooth setup and demo experience.

The product does not need to be huge. It needs to be complete, safe, useful, and well presented.

## Non-Negotiable Product Expectations

The product should never intentionally:

- Expose one user's private work to another user.
- Give users access to actions they should not have.
- Hide important errors behind vague messages.
- Make destructive actions too easy to trigger by accident.
- Depend on unclear or undocumented behavior during a demo.
- Prioritize flashy features over trust and usefulness.

## Tone And Personality

The product should communicate in a tone that is:

- Clear
- Helpful
- Professional
- Calm
- Honest
- Respectful

Avoid language that is overly playful, robotic, vague, or intimidating.

Good product messages should tell the user what happened, what it means, and what they can do next.

## Guiding Question For Every Change

Before making any change, ask:

> Does this make the product more secure, useful, clear, accessible, or demo-ready for the customer?

If the answer is no, the change should be reconsidered.
